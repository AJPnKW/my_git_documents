What Venuiti Seems to Care About Most

  • Delivery discipline: on-time, on-budget, transparent execution.
  • Hands-on technical PM capability: not just steering decks, but real coordination across developers, technical staff, partners, testing, documentation, and implementation.
  • Integration and interoperability mindset: their public positioning is heavily tied to custom software, systems talking to each other, and healthcare interoperability.
  • Problem-solving over bureaucracy: their public language is direct, practical, and speed-oriented, not overly corporate.
  • A small-company operating style: fewer layers, more ownership, more proximity to builders, and less tolerance for passive PM behavior.
  • Regulated, meaningful software: they mention compliance and security, but as part of building useful, reliable healthcare technology rather than as a standalone governance function.

Your Strongest Fit Signals

  • 25+ years of delivery-adjacent work across enterprise applications, ERP, internet releases, HRMS, claims, benefits, and consulting environments.
  • Developer-facing project leadership across software, ERP, upgrade, migration, and implementation work.
  • Proof in regulated settings without sounding like a compliance-only candidate.
  • Good small-company signal from Hubble, Kuntz, Spaenaur, Best Software, and client-consulting work.
  • Strong recovery / stabilization stories for under-structured work, vendor projects, and operationally sensitive delivery.
  • Technical enough to talk with builders across ERP, databases, APIs, reporting, servers, and testing contexts.

Most Likely Top Questions

  • Walk me through your background and what makes you relevant for this role.
  • Why Venuiti and why this role?
  • Tell me about your software development or technical PM experience.
  • How have you managed scope, schedule, and stakeholder coordination?
  • How do you work with developers and technical teams?
  • Why the recent sabbatical, and what have you been doing?

Likely Decision Filters

  • Do you feel too enterprise or too big-company for a sub-50-person firm?
  • Can you speak software and implementation, not just governance?
  • Will you be practical and hands-on enough?
  • Are you strong with documentation, reporting, and follow-through?
  • Can you handle onsite work, pace, and ambiguity?
  • Do you genuinely want health-tech, or are you applying broadly?

Recommended Interview Style

  • Direct and practical, not polished-for-polish’s-sake.
  • Specific examples with outcomes, not generic leadership language.
  • Small-company aware: emphasize hands-on ownership and low ego.
  • Keep answers tight first, then deepen only when asked.
  • Frame risk/compliance as a delivery-strengthener, not your main identity.

1. Walk me through your background.

Best default move: answer in three beats: long-term pattern, most relevant TPM examples, then why that points naturally to Venuiti.

Approach A - Best overall for Venuiti 9.6 / 10

I have spent most of my career working where software delivery, enterprise systems, and real business operations meet. A lot of that work sat inside project or program roles, but it was rarely just schedule tracking. It involved coordinating developers, business leads, vendors, operational teams, and sometimes offshore resources to move application and platform work from plan to execution without losing control of scope, timing, or documentation.

The strongest examples for this role are probably Sun Life, Manulife, Hubble, Kuntz, and Spaenaur. At Sun Life and Manulife I managed application and business-systems initiatives in structured enterprise environments. At Hubble and Kuntz I was closer to ERP delivery, implementation, upgrades, training, change control, and client-facing execution. At Spaenaur I was leading applications, development, support, and portfolio priorities more broadly. So even though some later roles leaned more into risk and compliance, the long pattern underneath has been technical delivery, systems thinking, and getting complex work across the line.

That is a big reason this role makes sense to me. It gets back to the part of my background that has always been core: working closely with technical teams, managing delivery, solving issues early, and helping software and integration work move with structure and follow-through.

Approach B - More concise and recruiter-friendly 9.1 / 10

My background is a mix of technical project management, enterprise applications, ERP and systems delivery, and regulated-environment execution. Over time I have led software, application, and business-systems work across insurance, manufacturing, consulting, and technology environments. The common thread has been coordinating people, plans, issues, and deliverables so that the work actually lands.

The most relevant pieces for Venuiti are my application and software-delivery work at Sun Life and Manulife, my ERP consulting and implementation work at Hubble and Kuntz, and my broader IT leadership at Spaenaur. Those roles gave me a lot of experience with developers, business stakeholders, vendors, reporting, testing support, documentation, and delivery controls.

Approach C - Slightly more distinctive, less standard 8.6 / 10

My career has never really lived in a single lane. The through-line has been stepping into environments where the software or systems work mattered, the moving parts were not simple, and somebody needed to bring structure, translation, and forward momentum. Sometimes the title was explicitly project management. Sometimes it leaned more technical, operational, or risk-oriented. But the work itself kept coming back to the same things: coordination, execution, problem-solving, and follow-through.

2. Why Venuiti, and why this role?

Approach A - Best overall 9.7 / 10

A few things stand out to me about Venuiti. First, the company is clearly close to the work. The way you talk about custom software, integration, testing, documentation, and support feels practical rather than overly corporate. Second, the healthcare angle matters. I like work that has a real use case and real end users behind it, and software that improves access, coordination, or day-to-day outcomes is more meaningful than building something abstract. Third, the size is appealing. In a smaller company, the work is usually closer to the product, the team, and the decisions, and that fits how I like to operate.

The role itself lines up with the part of my background I want to keep leaning into: technical project management in environments where software delivery, integrations, documentation, stakeholder coordination, and regulated requirements all matter at the same time. I also think my background gives me a useful extra dimension for a healthcare software setting, because I bring delivery discipline without turning everything into process theatre.

Approach B - Slightly shorter and safer 9.2 / 10

I am interested in Venuiti because the role sits at the intersection of software delivery, integrations, stakeholder coordination, and meaningful healthcare work. The company also feels smaller, more practical, and closer to the actual work than many larger environments, which is a better fit for how I like to operate. The position lines up well with my background in technical project delivery, enterprise applications, and regulated implementation work.

Approach C - More personal and values-led 8.8 / 10

I am at my best in environments where the software matters, the delivery matters, and the people using it matter. Venuiti seems to sit in that space. The role is not just about pushing status. It looks like a role where somebody needs to help the work move, keep people aligned, and make sure the details hold together. That is the kind of work I have done for a long time, and it is also the kind of work I still enjoy.

Approach B - More compact 9.1 / 10

I have led planning, coordination, reporting, vendor management, testing support, and implementation work across environments like Manulife, Sun Life, Hubble, Kuntz, and Spaenaur. Approach C - More strategic angle 8.5 / 10

My technical PM strength is less about one stack and more about being able to operate effectively across software, systems, integrations, and delivery disciplines. I have repeatedly worked in environments where the technology was important, the dependencies were real, and the work needed more than just administrative project management. My value there has been understanding enough of the technical context to keep the right people aligned and the work moving.

4. How have you managed scope, schedule, budget, and reporting?

Approach A - Best practical answer 9.7 / 10

I have usually managed that through a combination of front-end clarity and steady rhythm. Up front, I try to get clear on objectives, deliverables, key assumptions, constraints, dependencies, decision points, and who actually owns what. Then I build that into a working plan with milestones, risks, reporting cadence, and change handling, rather than treating the plan as a static artifact.

In practice that meant integrated project plans at Sun Life across scope, schedule, cost, quality, risk, communication, and procurement. It meant governance, change, risk, and release management standards at Manulife. It meant formal statements of work, requirements gathering, and change control at Hubble. And at Kuntz it meant introducing PMBOK-based project discipline where it was needed to improve project control.

The reporting side is important too. I am used to dashboards, status updates, milestone tracking, escalation when needed, and keeping stakeholders informed early enough that they can still affect the outcome.

Approach B - Shorter answer 9.0 / 10

I manage those areas by making the plan real and keeping it live. That means clear scope, milestones, owners, dependencies, reporting cadence, and change decisions from the start, then actively maintaining that through execution. A lot of my background at Sun Life, Manulife, Hubble, and Kuntz involved exactly that kind of delivery discipline.

Approach C - More outcome-led 8.7 / 10

I tend to think of scope, schedule, budget, and reporting as one system rather than four separate things. If any one of them is weak, the others stop being reliable. So I try to make the real trade-offs visible early and keep reporting honest enough that people can act before the project drifts too far.

5. How do you work with developers, technical teams, and stakeholders?

Approach A - Best overall 9.8 / 10

I try to be useful to both sides. With developers and technical teams, that usually means helping reduce noise, clarify priorities, surface dependencies, tighten decisions, and protect the team from avoidable confusion. With stakeholders, it means translating what is happening in a way that is accurate, understandable, and actionable without creating false certainty.

I have done that in different ways across internet releases at Manulife, ERP consulting at Hubble, application and portfolio work at Sun Life, and enterprise applications at Spaenaur. The pattern is consistent: keep communication clear, bring issues up early, make sure decisions get made, and do not let important details disappear between business and technical groups.

Approach B - More collaborative tone 9.2 / 10

I work best as a bridge. Developers, business leads, vendors, and stakeholders usually do not need more noise from a project manager. They need clarity, sequencing, and someone who keeps the commitments, trade-offs, and issues visible. That has been a big part of my role in delivery environments for years.

Approach C - More distinctive 8.9 / 10

I try to remove friction. A lot of project problems are not really technical problems or business problems on their own. They are coordination problems. I tend to be strongest where I can help technical teams stay focused, help stakeholders stay informed, and keep both sides from drifting apart.

7. Tell me about a time you stepped into an under-structured or troubled project.

Approach A - Best story choice 9.6 / 10

One good example is a business-initiated outsourced software project at Manulife that I took over and brought into a more disciplined delivery model. The issue was not that people were not working hard. It was that the structure around the work was not strong enough: planning, governance, communication, and visibility needed to be tightened.

I stepped in, aligned the work to IS best practices, brought the right departments together, clarified roles and deliverables, and re-established a more reliable execution rhythm. The outcome was better data quality, better customer turnaround times, and a project that had a stronger chance of landing well because the underlying delivery model was improved, not just the status reporting.

Approach B - Alternate story from Hubble/Kuntz 9.1 / 10

Another example is Hubble, where I introduced formal project methodology, statements of work, requirements gathering, and change control in an environment that needed stronger delivery discipline. That helped improve project completion on time, on budget, and on scope by roughly 50 percent. What mattered there was not process for its own sake. It was giving the work enough structure to finish well.

Approach C - More general and portable 8.4 / 10

I have stepped into that pattern multiple times. Usually the first signs are unclear ownership, issues getting discovered too late, and reporting that is technically happening but not actually helping decisions. My approach is to simplify the path forward, make dependencies visible, establish a real cadence, and get decisions back onto the table quickly.

10. Why a smaller company after larger enterprises?

Approach A - Best answer for fit 9.6 / 10

I have worked in both larger and smaller environments, and there are things I value in each. What I like about smaller companies is the closeness to the work. There is usually less distance between the problem, the people solving it, and the decisions that matter. That tends to suit me well because I am most effective when I can stay close to execution, help unblock issues quickly, and work directly with the people building and delivering.

I also do have smaller-company and consulting experience to draw on, not just big-enterprise experience. Hubble, Kuntz, Spaenaur, Best Software, and client consulting all required a more practical and less layered way of operating. So this would not be a new mode for me. It would be a return to one I know well.

Approach B - More compact 9.1 / 10

I do not see this as moving down in scale. I see it as moving closer to the work. Some of the environments where I have done my best work were smaller, more direct, and more execution-focused. That is part of what attracts me here.

Approach C - More distinctive and candid 8.6 / 10

I like environments where ownership is visible and progress is tangible. Smaller companies usually make both of those things more real. I think that tends to bring out stronger work from me.
